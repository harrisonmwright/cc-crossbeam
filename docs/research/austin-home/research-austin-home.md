# Austin, Texas — HOME Initiative Research

> **Research date**: March 2026
> **Subject**: Austin's HOME (Home Options for More Efficiency) Initiative — Phase 1 and Phase 2 Land Development Code amendments
> **Sources**: City of Austin DSD, AIA Austin, Texas Tribune, Austin Monitor, Austin Bulldog, Community Impact, KUT, BBK Law
> **Purpose**: Reference document for understanding Austin's approach to residential density reform; comparison context for CrossBeam's California ADU work

---

## Overview

Austin's HOME Initiative is a series of amendments to the City of Austin Land Development Code (LDC), Chapter 25-2, that effectively ends exclusionary single-family zoning citywide. The initiative was championed by Council Member Leslie Pool and aimed at expanding housing types in single-family zones, reducing barriers to missing-middle housing construction, and addressing Austin's acute housing affordability crisis.

**Applies to:** SF-1, SF-2, SF-3, and SF-4A/B zoning districts. Does NOT apply to Lake Austin zoning, Rural Residential, or Planned Unit Developments (PUDs).

**Official source:** https://www.austintexas.gov/page/home-amendments

---

## Phase 1 — More Units Per Lot

**Ordinance:** No. 20231207-001
**Adopted:** December 7, 2023
**Applications accepted:** February 5, 2024

### What It Changed

- **Up to 3 units by right** on any SF-zoned property (SF-1, SF-2, SF-3), including tiny homes — no discretionary approval required
- **Eliminated primary/secondary distinction** — removed "guest house," "on-site employee housing," and "accessory apartment" as separate use categories; any unit is now just a unit
- **Removed unrelated adult occupancy limits** — prior code capped co-habitation at 4 unrelated adults; eliminated entirely
- **Preservation bonus** — if ≥50% of an existing structure built 1960 or earlier is preserved AND 100% of the street-facing facade is retained, owner may build one additional unit beyond the zone's base limit. Preserved dwelling square footage is excluded from FAR calculations.
- **Tiny homes recognized as dwelling units** — units ≤400 sq ft (excluding loft space) may be classified as dwelling units. RVs remain excluded.
- **McMansion/Subchapter F** — limited to single-unit development only; does not apply to 2- or 3-unit projects

### Development Standards (Phase 1)

| Standard | Requirement |
|----------|------------|
| Units per lot | Up to 3 |
| Minimum lot size (2–3 units) | 2,500 sq ft |
| FAR (2 units) | ~0.55 |
| FAR (3 units) | ~0.65 |
| Building coverage | 40% (SF-1 aligned to match SF-2/SF-3) |
| Impervious cover | 45% |
| Front yard setback | 15 ft |
| Side/rear setback | 5 ft (if adjacent to alley or multifamily district) |
| Height | Up to 30 ft (2 stories) |
| Parking | No minimums (eliminated citywide Nov. 2, 2023) |
| Owner-occupancy requirement | None |
| STR (duplex/two-unit) | One unit may be used as STR |
| STR (ADU built after Oct. 1, 2015) | No more than 30 days/year |

**Note on triplexes:** For a triplex (all 3 units in a single attached building), a Request for Modification/Alternate Method of Compliance (AMOC) form must also be submitted per Policy Memo PM2024-0002. Not required for detached configurations.

## Phase 2 — Smaller Lots, More Parcels

**Ordinance:** No. 20240516-006
**Adopted:** May 16, 2024 (9–2 vote; Council Members Alter and Kelly dissenting)
**Applications accepted:** August 16, 2024 (phased; full citywide Nov. 16, 2024)

### What It Changed

- **New land use category:** "small lot single-family residential use" in SF-1, SF-2, SF-3
- **Minimum lot size for 1 unit** reduced from **5,750 sq ft → 1,800 sq ft** (a 68.7% reduction from the standard that had stood since 1946). Staff had recommended 2,000 sq ft; Planning Commission recommended 1,500 sq ft; Council adopted 1,800 sq ft.
- **Minimum lot size for 2–3 units** unchanged at 2,500 sq ft (Phase 1 baseline)
- **McMansion/Subchapter F** removed entirely for small lots (<5,750 sq ft)
- **Interior lot line setbacks** removed between newly created small lots
- **Flag lot regulations** adjusted — flagpole reduced to 15 ft (dedicated driveway) or 10 ft (shared/no driveway) to facilitate subdivision
- **Compatibility standards** (adopted alongside Phase 2): height restriction reach capped at 75 ft citywide, down from a prior 540-ft reach dating to the 1980s — projected to unlock 71,000+ additional housing units
- **STR on small lots:** Temporary ban included
- **Parking:** No change — no minimums citywide

### Application Paths

#### 1. Subdivision Applications (for lots ≥5,750 sq ft)

Most existing Austin lots exceed the standard minimum. To use Phase 2 on these, the owner must first subdivide via the **Residential Infill Subdivision** process (a new streamlined process created specifically for HOME Phase 2). To qualify:

- Site must not exceed 1 acre
- Must be a resubdivision/replat (not raw land)
- Must include only land already platted as a residential subdivision
- Must not require a plat vacation

#### 2. Building Permit Applications (for lots already <5,750 sq ft)

Existing undersized lots skip subdivision and apply HOME Phase 2 development regulations directly via a building permit to establish small lot single-family residential use.

### Phased Rollout

| Date | Coverage |
|------|----------|
| Aug. 16, 2024 | All areas except WUI and Uprooted Report-designated displacement-risk areas |
| Nov. 16, 2024 | Full citywide, including WUI and Uprooted Report areas |

The delay for Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI) areas was due to wildfire concerns. The delay for Uprooted Report areas allowed anti-displacement tools to be finalized before permitting potentially speculative redevelopment pressure on vulnerable communities (the Uprooted Report was originally a 2018 UT study, updated by the city in 2024).

## Site Plan Lite Phase 2 & Infill Plat (March 2025)

**Adopted:** March 6, 2025
**Applications accepted:** June 16, 2025

A third wave of the HOME reform package bridging HOME Phase 1/2 (1–4 units) and full site plan review:

- **Scope:** 5–16 unit residential projects; re-subdivisions creating up to 8 new lots on parcels ≤1 acre
- **Cost savings:** Up to 40% reduction in project costs by eliminating micro-pond (onsite detention) requirements for projects under 8,000 sq ft of impervious cover
- **Drainage reform:** Tied impervious cover assumptions directly to zoning-allowed cover
- **Permit goal:** 90-day turnaround target

## Permit Process

**Processed by:** Austin Development Services Department (DSD), Residential Plan Review division
**Address:** Permitting and Development Center (PDC), 6310 Wilhelmina Delco Dr., Austin, TX 78752
**Zoning appointments:** ResidentialAppointment@austintexas.gov

### How to Apply

- **2- or 3-unit project (Phase 1):** Residential New Construction and Addition Permit Application via Expedited Residential Review
- **True triplex (3 attached units):** Also submit AMOC form per PM2024-0002
- **Phase 2 subdivision (lot ≥5,750 sq ft):** Residential Infill Subdivision application
- **Phase 2 building permit (lot already <5,750 sq ft):** Standard building permit with Phase 2 development regulations

### Key Requirements

- New units require a unique address from Address Management Services before permit application
- Compliance reviews: LDC Ch. 25-2, 2024 IRC, Ch. 25-12 Technical Codes (local amendments), tree ordinance (19"+ diameter trees), landscaping (3+ unit projects or large duplexes)
- 2024 IECC energy code effective July 10, 2025 — includes EV charging readiness requirements
- Austin Energy's ECAD ordinance applies to residential buildings with ≤4 units
- **Drainage:** For lots created before June 16, 2025, no drainage review required for ≤4 units

### Timelines

| Type | Target |
|------|--------|
| Simple residential permit | A few weeks |
| Standard HOME permit | 10 business days (target); 4–6 weeks in practice |
| Phase 2 infill subdivision | 90 days (target) |

## Implementation Status

**Overall:** Both phases active and accepting applications statewide (citywide) as of November 16, 2024.

**Early permit activity:**
- Phase 1 saw meaningful early adoption concentrated in areas already experiencing redevelopment pressure
- Phase 2 had limited early uptake — only 8 total applications (2 subdivisions, 6 building permits) as of early February 2025, attributed partly to Austin's complex and expensive subdivision review process

**Notable refinements:**
- October 2025: Clarification on four-unit developments under HOME
- December 2025: Stairs count toward gross floor area on first floor only for 2- and 3-unit HOME projects; Site Plan Exemption no longer required for 3- and 4-unit projects for landscaping review
- February 2026: AI Pre-Check beta launched (powered by Archistar) for standard residential building plan review

## Legal Challenges

**HOME amendments (Phase 1 and 2) have not been successfully invalidated as of March 2026.**

**Acuña v. City of Austin (Community Not Commodity):** An ongoing lawsuit by neighborhood group Community Not Commodity that successfully overturned three 2022 zoning ordinances (VMU2, Residential in Commercial, and Compatibility) on procedural grounds — the city failed to provide required written notice before changing zoning rules. The core issue was notice procedure, not policy substance. Council re-adopted those ordinances using proper notice procedures. The group warned they were prepared to return to court over HOME and related code changes but no ruling has invalidated the HOME amendments.

**Deed restriction conflicts:** A separate legal tension emerged over deed restrictions. Some properties have restrictions limiting units per lot. When Phase 2 enabled subdivision, developers argued deed restrictions should be interpreted proportionally for new smaller lots. This interpretation has been contested in court with attorneys warning the city could face class action litigation. Courts have begun hearing deed restriction cases related to HOME.

## Comparison to California ADU Law (Gov. Code §§ 66310–66342)

Both Austin HOME and California's ADU statutes tackle the same problem — housing supply in single-family zones — through different mechanisms.

| Feature | Austin HOME | California §§ 66310–66342 |
|---------|-------------|--------------------------|
| **Legal mechanism** | Local city ordinance (city-initiated) | State law preempting local ordinances |
| **Geography** | City of Austin only | All of California statewide |
| **Override** | Austin City Council can amend or repeal | Only Legislature can change; HCD enforces |
| **Units per SF lot** | Up to 3 (co-equal) | Up to 3: 1 primary + 1 ADU + 1 JADU |
| **Primary/accessory distinction** | Eliminated — all units equal | Retained — ADU/JADU are accessory uses |
| **Owner-occupancy** | None required | Prohibited for ADUs; required for JADUs (if shared sanitation, per AB 1154) |
| **Minimum lot size** | 1,800 sq ft (1 unit); 2,500 sq ft (2–3 units) | No minimum — must allow 800 sq ft ADU on any lot regardless of size or FAR |
| **Max unit size** | Governed by FAR (~1,100–1,350 sq ft typical) | At least 800–1,000 sq ft guaranteed; max generally 1,200 sq ft or 50% of primary |
| **FAR** | 0.55 (2-unit); 0.65 (3-unit) | Must allow 800+ sq ft notwithstanding FAR limits |
| **Height** | Up to 30–35 ft (2 stories) | 16 ft minimum always; 18 ft near transit; up to 25 ft in some cases |
| **Setbacks** | Front: 15 ft; Side/rear: 5 ft; no setbacks between new small lots (Phase 2) | Rear and side: 4 ft max; no front setback for conversion ADUs |
| **Parking** | No minimums (eliminated citywide) | No more than 1 space/unit or bedroom; exempt near transit, conversions, car-share |
| **Permit timeline** | 10 business days target; 4–6 weeks in practice | 60-day shot clock; 15 business days for completeness; deemed approved if not acted on |
| **Discretionary review** | Ministerial (by-right) | Ministerial only; no public hearing permitted |
| **Impact fees** | Local fees apply | No fees for ADUs ≤750 sq ft; proportional for larger (§ 66311.5) |
| **Lot subdivision** | Phase 2 enables actual lot splitting | SB 9 / § 66411.7 allows SF lot splitting (more constrained) |
| **Condo sale** | Via condo regime (all units, one lot) | AB 1033 / § 66342 allows local opt-in for separate ADU condo sale |
| **Unpermitted amnesty** | No equivalent | Cannot deny permit for ADUs built before Jan. 1, 2020 |
| **State backstop** | None — Texas has no statewide housing preemption | Complete preemption — non-compliant local ordinances are void |
| **Pre-approved plans** | AI Pre-Check beta (Feb. 2026) | Required by AB 1332 (eff. Jan. 1, 2024) |
| **Enforcement** | Austin DSD (local) | HCD (state-level, comprehensive since Jan. 1, 2024) |

### Key Differences

1. **Enforcement backstop:** California's HCD can declare non-compliant local ordinances null and void and impose $10K–$50K/month fines. Austin HOME is purely local — a future City Council could amend or repeal it.

2. **Lot subdivision:** HOME Phase 2 enables genuine parcel splitting. California's SB 9 is the rough equivalent but more constrained in lot width minimums and urban lot size requirements.

3. **ADU vs. full unit:** California ADU law creates an *accessory* use layered on the primary dwelling. Austin HOME eliminated the accessory distinction entirely — all units are co-equal. This has significant financing, insurance, and valuation implications.

4. **Permit shot clock:** California has a hard 60-day statutory deadline with automatic deemed-approval consequences if missed. Austin's is a target only.

5. **Scope:** California's law is ADU-specific and detailed (28+ reference files in the california-adu skill). Austin HOME is a broader land use reform with less prescriptive detail at the code level.

### Relevance to CrossBeam

1. **Market signal:** Austin is the largest U.S. city to end single-family exclusivity. The policy direction is clear — permit infrastructure is the lagging edge. CrossBeam's California focus is in the same direction.

2. **Texas expansion:** HOME Phase 2's subdivision + building permit two-step creates exactly the kind of multi-process complexity CrossBeam is designed to solve. Texas has no statewide ADU law, so city-by-city complexity would be higher, not lower.

3. **Comparison selling point:** California homeowners and designers who feel state ADU law is restrictive can reference Austin as a benchmark — California's fee exemptions, HCD enforcement, and JADU framework are actually more mature than Austin's approach.

4. **AI Pre-Check (Feb. 2026):** Austin just launched an AI plan pre-check beta powered by Archistar. This is a direct analog to CrossBeam's value proposition in a non-California market — confirms the product category is real and emerging.
