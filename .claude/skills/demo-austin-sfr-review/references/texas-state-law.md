# Texas State Law — SFR Zoning and Land Use

Applicable Texas statutes from the 89th Legislature (2025) that affect single-family and small-lot residential development in Austin, TX.

**Applicability of all statutes below:** Austin qualifies under the population threshold (population >150,000 in a county with population >300,000).

---

## Preemption Principle

Texas state law sets a **floor** — it tells municipalities the most restrictive they can be. Austin's HOME Initiative ordinances are often **more permissive** than the state floor. When both apply:

- If Austin HOME allows something the state floor also allows: apply Austin HOME rules
- If Austin HOME allows something more permissive than the state floor: Austin HOME governs
- If a city LDC provision is **more restrictive** than the state floor: the state law **preempts** — do not cite the more restrictive local provision as a valid requirement
- **Do not issue a correction based on a local rule that state law has preempted**

## SB 15 (2025) — Residential Lot Size and Density

**Citation:** Tex. Local Gov't Code, Chapter 211, Subchapter D (§§211.051–211.058)
**Effective:** September 1, 2025
**Applies to:** New plats (tracts of 5+ acres being platted for SFR with no recorded plat) in qualifying municipalities

### Key Definitions
- **Small lot:** A residential lot that is 4,000 sq ft or less

### What SB 15 Prohibits (Cities May NOT Require)

**For any residential lot:**
| Requirement | Maximum the city can impose |
|-------------|---------------------------|
| Minimum lot size | Greater than **3,000 sq ft** |
| Minimum lot width | Greater than **30 ft** |
| Minimum lot depth | Greater than **75 ft** |
| Density | Any ratio that prevents SFH on a lot ≥3,000 sq ft |

**For small lots (≤4,000 sq ft), additional prohibitions:**
| Requirement | Rule |
|-------------|------|
| Front setback | May not exceed **15 ft** |
| Rear setback | May not exceed **10 ft** |
| Side setback | May not exceed **5 ft** |
| Covered parking | **Prohibited** requirement |
| Parking spaces per unit | May not require more than **1** |
| Off-site parking | **Prohibited** requirement |
| Open space / permeable surface | May not require more than **30%** |
| Stories | May not require fewer than **3 full stories** (≤10 ft floor-to-ceiling per story) |
| Maximum building bulk | **Prohibited** restriction |
| Wall articulation | **Prohibited** requirement |
| Inconsistent restrictions via contiguous zoning / overlay districts | **Prohibited** |

### What SB 15 Allows (Cities MAY Still Require)
- Sharing a driveway with another lot
- Permitting fees equivalent to those for a standard SFR lot
- Impact fees authorized under Chapter 395
- Setbacks related to environmental features, erosion, or waterways (to extent authorized by federal/other state law)
- Aquifer protection measures in aquifer recharge zones (notwithstanding the 30% open space limit)

### Interaction with Austin HOME

| Topic | Austin HOME Rule | SB 15 State Floor | Which Governs |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|---------------|
| Minimum lot size | Phase II: 1,800 sq ft | 3,000 sq ft max minimum | **Austin HOME** (more permissive — allows smaller lots than state floor) |
| Minimum lot width | Phase II: 15 ft | 30 ft max minimum | **Austin HOME** (more permissive) |
| Front setback (small lots) | Phase II: 10 ft (or 5 ft for flag lots) | Max 15 ft requirement | **Austin HOME** (more permissive) |
| Side setback (small lots) | Phase II: 5 ft (or 0 ft in some cases) | Max 5 ft requirement | Equal — both allow 5 ft; HOME allows less in some cases |
| Rear setback (small lots) | Phase II: 5 ft (or 0 ft in some cases) | Max 10 ft requirement | **Austin HOME** (more permissive) |
| Covered parking | Phase I/II: not explicitly prohibited | Prohibited requirement | **SB 15** — cannot require covered parking for small lots |
| Parking per unit | Not explicitly limited by HOME | Max 1 per unit for small lots | **SB 15** preempts any local rule requiring 2+ spaces on small lots |

### Enforcement
- Persons adversely affected or housing organizations may bring civil action against the municipality
- Court may: enter declaratory judgment, issue writ of mandamus, issue injunction
- Prevailing claimant awarded attorney's fees and court costs
- May elect 15th Court of Appeals as exclusive intermediate appellate court

## SB 840 (2025) — By-Right Mixed-Use and Multifamily Residential

**Citation:** Tex. Local Gov't Code, Chapter 218
**Effective:** September 1, 2025
**Applies to:** New development projects and conversion permits filed on or after September 1, 2025

### What SB 840 Requires (By-Right Allowance)

Any zoning classification that allows **office, commercial, retail, warehouse, or mixed-use** development **must also allow** mixed-use residential and multifamily residential **by right**, without requiring:
- Rezoning
- Zoning variance or exception
- Special use permit
- Conditional use permit
- Comprehensive plan amendment
- Any other discretionary approval

### Definition of Mixed-Use Residential
- Residential + nonresidential uses where **residential ≥ 65%** of total sq footage
- Includes condominium development

### Definition of Multifamily Residential
- 3 or more dwelling units within one or more buildings
- Includes residential condominium

### Development Standards — What Cities May NOT Impose on Mixed-Use/Multifamily

| Standard | Restriction |
|----------|-------------|
| Density limit | Cannot be more restrictive than the greater of: (i) highest residential density allowed in municipality, or (ii) **36 units per acre** |
| Height limit | Cannot be more restrictive than the greater of: (i) highest height allowed for office/commercial/retail/warehouse on site, or (ii) **45 ft** |
| Setback/buffer | Cannot be more restrictive than the lesser of: (i) setback applying to office/commercial/retail/warehouse on same site, or (ii) **25 ft** |
| Parking | Cannot require more than **1 parking space per dwelling unit** |
| Parking structure | Cannot require a **multilevel parking structure** |
| FAR | Cannot restrict **floor-area ratio** |
| Mixed-use requirement | Cannot require a multifamily development (not in a mixed-use zone) to contain nonresidential uses |

### Ministerial Approval
If a proposed mixed-use or multifamily project meets municipal land development regulations under SB 840, the reviewing authority **shall administratively approve** the permit — no governing body action required.

### Exceptions (SB 840 Does NOT Apply To)
- Heavy industrial use zones
- Land within 1,000 ft of existing heavy industrial use
- Land within 3,000 ft of an airport or military base
- Clear zones and accident potential zones

### Conversion of Office/Retail/Warehouse to Residential (Subchapter C)
For buildings being converted that are ≥5 years old and where ≥65% of total sq footage and each floor is converted to mixed-use or multifamily residential:

**Cities may NOT require:**
- Traffic impact analysis
- Traffic mitigation fees or construction
- Additional parking (existing spaces only)
- Utility extension/upgrade beyond minimum needed to serve the building
- Exterior design, window, internal environment, or interior space dimensions more restrictive than IBC minimum commercial building code standards
- Impact fees (if the land was not already subject to impact fees before the permit was filed)

### Plan Review Note
SB 840 is most relevant for **commercial or mixed-use plan sets** being converted to residential. For a standard SFR permit in an SF-zoned area, SB 840 is generally **not applicable**. It becomes relevant if reviewing a mixed-use or multifamily project in a commercially-zoned area.

## HB 24 (2025) — Zoning Change Procedures

**Citation:** Tex. Local Gov't Code §§211.0061, 211.0063, 211.0065, 211.0073
**Effective:** September 1, 2025

### Relevance to Plan Review
HB 24 primarily governs **how cities must process zoning changes** — it is **not directly applicable during a permit plan review** of an already-approved zoning. A permit reviewer checks compliance with the current applicable zoning; HB 24 governs the legislative process for changing that zoning.

### Key Changes (for context)

**Protest rights for non-comprehensive zoning changes** (individual parcel rezoning):
- Written protest signed by owners of ≥20% of affected area OR ≥20% of area within 200 ft requires ¾ supermajority of governing body to pass

**Reduced protest threshold for "comprehensive zoning changes"** (upzoning across districts):
- A comprehensive zoning change (allows more residential across a zoning district or overlay along a major corridor) can be protested only at 60% threshold within 200 ft (vs. 20%)
- Requires only a majority of governing body (not ¾) if protested at the 60% level

**60-day challenge window:**
- A zoning change allowing more residential is **conclusively presumed valid** if no legal challenge is filed within 60 days of effective date

**Notice signs:**
- Signs required at least 10 days before zoning commission hearing for non-citywide zoning changes

## Notes for Plan Checkers

1. **SB 15 effective date is September 1, 2025.** For permits submitted before that date, SB 15 does not apply — use only Austin LDC and HOME rules.

2. **Do not issue a correction based on a local rule that state law preempts.** If a plan complies with the state floor but not the (more restrictive) local rule, note the state preemption and mark PASS.

3. **Austin HOME is generally more permissive than SB 15.** In most cases the Austin HOME rules govern because they allow smaller lots, narrower lots, and reduced setbacks beyond what SB 15 requires. SB 15 primarily protects the developer from a city trying to reimpose the old more-restrictive rules.

4. **SB 840 is not typically relevant for a standard SFR permit.** Flag it only if the plan involves a mixed-use or multifamily project, or a conversion from commercial use.

5. **HB 24 is a legislative procedure law, not a development standard.** It does not create any compliance items in a plan check.
